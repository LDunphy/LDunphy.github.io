1. Data Foundations

This section covers the fundamental concepts of data. Understanding these basics is crucial for all other topics in data science. We will explore the different types of data, how they are measured, and how they are structured. The interactive elements here will help you solidify your understanding through active participation.

Quantitative vs. Qualitative Data

Quantitative

Data that can be measured numerically. It answers questions like "how many?", "how much?", or "how often?". This data is objective and can be used in mathematical calculations.

  • Discrete: Whole numbers (e.g., number of students).
  • Continuous: Can take any value within a range (e.g., height, temperature).

Qualitative

Descriptive data that is observed, not measured. It answers "why?" or "how?". It deals with descriptions, categories, and characteristics, providing context and depth.

  • Nominal: Categories without order (e.g., eye colour).
  • Ordinal: Categories with a meaningful order (e.g., survey ratings 'good', 'average', 'bad').

Nominal

Categories with no order or ranking. Think of them as labels. Example: Types of pets (Dog, Cat, Fish), Gender, Postcodes.

Ordinal

Data can be ordered or ranked, but the differences between ranks are not equal or meaningful. Example: Survey ratings (Satisfied, Neutral, Dissatisfied), Education level (High School, Bachelor's, Master's).

Interval

Ordered data with equal intervals between values, but no true zero point. This means you can't say "twice as hot". Example: Temperature in Celsius/Fahrenheit, Years (e.g., 2023, 2024).

Ratio

The highest level of measurement. It has a true zero, equal intervals, and can be ordered. You can make ratio comparisons ("twice as tall"). Example: Height, Weight, Age, Price.

Structured vs. Unstructured Data

Structured Data

Highly organised data that fits neatly into a predefined model, like a table with rows and columns. It's easy to store, query, and analyse. Think of a spreadsheet or a relational database.

Unstructured Data

Data without a predefined structure. It's often text-heavy but can include images, videos, and audio. It's more difficult to process but contains rich insights. Think social media posts or emails.

2. Data Collection & Quality

This section explores how data is gathered and the critical importance of ensuring its quality. We'll look at different sampling methods and the criteria used to assess data: relevance, accuracy, validity, and reliability. Poor quality data leads to flawed conclusions, so these concepts are vital for any data-driven project.

Data Sampling

Active Collection

Data is collected with the subject's direct and knowing participation. The subject is actively providing the information.

  • Manual: Filling out a survey form, interviews.
  • Computerised: Completing an online poll, submitting a web form.

Passive Collection

Data is collected in the background, often without the subject's active involvement at the moment of collection.

  • Example: Website cookies tracking user behaviour, CCTV footage, GPS location tracking on a smartphone.

Assessing Data Quality (RAVR)

Primary data is collected firsthand for a specific purpose. Secondary data was collected by someone else for a different purpose. Both must be assessed for quality.

Relevance ‚ñº
Does the data directly relate to the problem you're trying to solve? Data can be accurate but completely irrelevant to your needs.
Accuracy ‚ñº
Is the data correct and free from errors? Consider the source, potential for typos, and whether it has been verified.
Validity ‚ñº
Does the data measure what it's supposed to measure? For example, using website visits to measure customer satisfaction might not be valid.
Reliability ‚ñº
Is the data collection process consistent? If you collected the data again using the same method, would you get similar results?

3. Storage & Management

Once collected, data needs to be stored and managed effectively. This section covers different storage methods, from your local hard drive to massive data warehouses in the cloud. We'll also examine the characteristics of "Big Data" and explore how Blockchain technology provides a secure way to manage and verify data.

Methods for Data Storage

Local Storage

Data stored on a device you physically own (e.g., computer hard drive, SSD). Fast access, no internet needed. Risky due to potential for device failure or theft.

Cloud Storage

Data stored on remote servers accessed via the internet (e.g., Google Drive, Dropbox). Accessible from anywhere, easily scalable. Relies on internet connection and third-party security.

Portable Media

Physical devices used to transport data (e.g., USB drives, external hard drives). Good for backups and transfers. Easily lost or damaged.

Data Warehouses

A large, centralized repository of data from various sources, designed for querying and analysis. Optimized for business intelligence, not for transaction processing.

Big Data: The Three V's

V

Volume

The sheer amount of data being generated, from terabytes to zettabytes.

V

Variety

The different types of data, including structured, unstructured, and semi-structured formats.

V

Velocity

The speed at which data is generated and needs to be processed, such as real-time streaming data.

Blockchain Technology

A blockchain is a decentralized, distributed, and immutable digital ledger used to record transactions across many computers. Its security and transparency make it useful for more than just cryptocurrencies.

How it Works:

Transactions are bundled into 'blocks'. Each block is cryptographically linked to the previous one, forming a 'chain'. This structure makes it extremely difficult to alter past transactions without being detected.

Applications:

  • Online Voting: Secure and transparent recording of votes to prevent fraud.
  • Online Identities: Gives individuals control over their digital identity, reducing reliance on central authorities.
  • Tracking Items of Value: Verifying the authenticity and tracking the supply chain of goods like diamonds or pharmaceuticals.
  • Recordkeeping: Creating unchangeable records for legal documents, medical histories, or property ownership.

Data Mining: Risks & Benefits

Data mining is the process of discovering patterns and other valuable information from large datasets. It has powerful benefits but also comes with significant risks.

Benefits

Businesses can predict customer behaviour, improve marketing, detect fraud, and optimize operations. In science, it helps in medical diagnosis and research.

Risks

Major privacy concerns arise from collecting personal data. It can lead to discrimination if used improperly (e.g., in loan applications). Inaccurate data can lead to flawed conclusions.

Impact of Data Scale

The sheer scale of modern data collection has transformative effects on technology, society, and ethics.

  • ‚ñ∫ Opportunities for Machine Learning (ML): Large datasets are the fuel for ML models, enabling them to learn complex patterns and make accurate predictions, from image recognition to language translation.
  • ‚ñ∫ Changes in Human Behaviour: Recommendation algorithms on platforms like Netflix and Amazon shape our choices. Personalized ads influence purchasing decisions. This creates filter bubbles and can limit exposure to diverse viewpoints.
  • ‚ñ∫ Ethical Implications: Your 'digital footprint' (every click, like, and search) creates a detailed profile of you. This raises questions about consent, ownership of data, and the potential for surveillance and manipulation.
